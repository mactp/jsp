The History of Jacob Smith Park

From ancient landscape and medieval farming to wartime training and a modern-day gift to the community.

A Brief History of the Park

by Kevin Earl

A research study has revealed that Jacob Smith Park holds significant local archaeological importance and is a relic of a much older landscape associated with the village of Scriven.

The Claro Community Archaeology Group (Knaresborough) published findings in 2013 in The Chronicles of Scriven. Low Wood at the western edge of the park was planted as part of 18th‑century landscaping by the Slingsby family. Their estate wall still runs along Scriven Road and Scotch George Lane. Scriven Hall, overlooking the park, was demolished in 1954 after a fire.

Archaeology: Guiseley Hill at the top of the park is a possible Iron Age settlement with bank and ditch earthworks and traces of hut circles. No excavations have taken place; all remains are undisturbed.

Ridge and furrow, evidence of medieval ploughing, can still be seen when the grass is short. A 1629 map shows a farmstead near Scotch George Lane. The ponds may relate to small‑scale iron smelting in the 16th–17th centuries and there is evidence of historic water management in the fields.

Scriven Through Time

  1. Medieval–Post‑Medieval

    Ridge & furrow agriculture; estate development under the Slingsby family.

  2. 18th Century

    Landscaping of parkland and planting of Low Wood; construction of the estate wall along Scriven Road and Scotch George Lane.

  3. 1954

    Scriven Hall demolished following fire damage.

  4. Second World War

    Park used for training tank crews and infantry while Scriven Hall served as an army camp.

  5. Village Life

    The park also hosted the village cricket pitch in the early–mid 20th century.

  6. 1955–56

    After the estate was sold, sisters Winifred and Dorothy Jacob Smith purchased the 30 acres that later became the park for grazing their Ayrshire cattle.

  7. 2003–2008

    Winifred Jacob Smith MBE bequeathed the land to the public; Jacob Smith Park opened officially in 2008.

Training Ground in Wartime

During WWII, Scriven Hall was requisitioned as an army camp and the park was used to train tank crews and infantry.

A Place for Sport

The park doubled as the village cricket ground. Teams and spectators gathered here for matches and local events.

History of the Family Who Granted Us the Freedom of the Park

“For people to enjoy the freedom and the beauty that public parks bring.”

— Winifred Jacob Smith MBE

Winifred Jacob Smith MBE

Winifred was born on 4 August 1911 to Dora and Jacob Smith, a farming family whose roots stretch back to 1710 at Humberton between Knaresborough and Boroughbridge, including farms on the Newby Hall Estate at Givendale and at Burton Grange.

Traditionally the first son in each generation was named Jacob. With daughters Winifred and Dorothy, their father wove the name into the family surname so the tradition lived on. Dora was president of the Knaresborough Women’s Institute and, along with her daughters, played a leading role in the farm and community.

The Jacob Smith family’s story is an important part of Knaresborough’s history. The timeline below highlights milestones in the lives of one of the town’s most respected families.

  1. 18 May 1910

    Dorothy Smith is born.

  2. 4 August 1911

    Winifred Smith is born.

  3. c.1920 (post‑WWI)

    The family moves to Knaresborough; Jacob takes over Park Corner Farm, Scriven.

  4. 1937

    At the WI, Winifred meets Lady Celia Coates—asked to look after the Women’s Land Army should war break out.

  5. 1938

    Winifred & Dorothy publish a local recipe book to raise funds for the parish church.

  6. Sept 1939–1945

    Winifred (age 28) joins the Women’s Land Army and becomes organiser for North Yorkshire, later all Yorkshire—keeping detailed diaries and editing the monthly WLA Bulletin.

  7. Dec 1941

    Jacob (father) dies aged 74; Winifred & Dorothy run the dairy enterprise at Park Corner Farm with their pedigree Ayrshire herd.

  8. 28 Feb 1951

    Winifred is awarded the MBE for her WLA service at Buckingham Palace.

  9. 1965

    Winifred & Dorothy purchase 30 acres of Scriven Park (previously leased) to graze their cattle.

  10. 1974–1984

    Dora dies aged 100 (1974); Dorothy dies aged 73 (1984).

  11. 23 May 2003

    Winifred Jacob Smith MBE dies aged 91. Park bequeathed for public enjoyment; major legacy to Yorkshire Cancer Research.

  12. January 2008

    Jacob Smith Park (formerly Scriven Park) officially opens to the public.

  13. 2010

    Somerley, the family home, is demolished after falling into disrepair; Somerley Lane now marks the site.

Knaresborough Post — Archive Extracts

16 March 1940 — WLA reception by the Queen

Miss Winifred Jacob Smith … selected as one of the Women’s Land Army representatives to attend a reception by the Queen in London. Miss Smith has been acting as secretary for the North Riding branch of the Land Army.

15 June 1940 — Wartime cookery lectures

Miss Dorothy Jacob Smith delivers free wartime cookery classes to packed audiences at the WI and the Modern School, demonstrating nourishing dishes without rationed foods.

12 October 1940 — School potato‑picking

Pupils assist local farmers with the potato harvest at Scriven and Hopewell House; Mr Jacob Smith praises their efforts.

10 May 1941 — Services Club & Canteen

Miss Dorothy Jacob Smith appointed assistant organiser; committee funds support local regiments.
